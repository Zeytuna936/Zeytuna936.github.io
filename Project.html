Aim of the Project

Armed conflicts represent one of the most significant barriers to sustainable development. They weaken governance systems, create institutional inefficiencies, and contribute to rising levels of corruption and instability. Economic performance is also severely affected through higher unemployment and inflation, disruptions to supply chains, declining investment, and the displacement of capital. As a result, populations experience deteriorating living conditions, and many citizens choose to migrate abroad, leading to a loss of skilled labor and an increase in global migration flows.

Being known as the deadliest one in Europe since WW2, the Yugoslav Wars led to extensive human and economic devastation, including ethnic cleansing, genocide, and psychological trauma, alongside the breakup of Yugoslavia. Among the six successor states, Bosnia and Herzegovina (BH), Croatia, and Serbia were the most heavily affected, while Montenegro, North Macedonia (NM), and Slovenia gained independence largely through peaceful negotiations.

This project seeks to examine how the Yugoslav Wars influenced the development trajectories of the former Yugoslav countries, with particular attention to those that experienced direct conflict. The analysis incorporates key economic and social indicators to evaluate the main factors contributing to post-conflict development and long-term recovery.

Data Limitations

The availability of data across the six countries is uneven, particularly for BH, North Macedonia NM, and Montenegro. This limitation is most visible in the emissions dataset presented in Chart 5. Historical country-level information before the breakup of Yugoslavia is also scarce; for instance, the 1990 data points could not be included in Chart 2 due to gaps in the available series.

For Charts 3 and 6, fully comparable indicators for all countries were not accessible from official or high-reliability sources. As a result, supplementary data from Wikipedia were used to close missing gaps. Since the source contained multiple tables with inconsistent labels and unmarked sections, the initial Python extraction produced mixed results. To ensure accuracy and comparability, the datasets were manually reviewed and standardized in Excel before visualization.

Conclusion

Slovenia and Croatia demonstrate stronger development outcomes compared to the other countries in the region. Although Serbia records the second-highest GDP, it continues to face a relatively high unemployment rate when compared with Croatia and Slovenia. Bosnia and Herzegovina remains the most constrained, reflecting the extent of wartime damage, political fragmentation, and institutional challenges. The results for North Macedonia, which is landlocked, and Montenegro, which relies heavily on tourism, are consistent with their structural characteristics.

While it is difficult to establish a direct causal link between current performance and the legacy of conflict, the long-term effects appear most visible in Bosnia and Herzegovina. Serbia, with its stronger institutional base and strategic ties to eastern partners, and Croatia, with closer alignment to the European Union and a robust tourism sector, have both made greater progress in overcoming post-conflict barriers.
