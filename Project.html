Aim of the Project

Armed conflicts are one of the fundamental obstacles to progress- they hinder development through weakened governance (which leads to inefficient institutions and a rise of corruption and instability) and disruption to the economy (which includes an increase in unemployment and inflation, damage to supply chain, fall of investment, and displacement of capital). Severely affected by the detrimental impacts of war, citizens either continue their lives under heavy living conditions or choose to move abroad which results in displacement of skilled labor and increase in global migration.

Being known as the deadliest one in Europe since WW2, the Yugoslav Wars resulted in ethnic cleansing, genocide, and psychological trauma along with the breakup of Yugoslavia. Among 6 former Yugoslavian countries, some of them were affected by the bloody wars the most (Bosnia and Herzegovina (BH), Croatia, Serbia) while some of them could get independence under peace and negotiation (Montenegro, North Macedonia (NM), Slovenia).

In my research, I intend to investigate to what extent the Yugoslav Wars hindered development of former Yugoslavian countries, especially the ones that experienced it the most. To analyze it, the project aims to include indicators of main factors contributing to development among these countries.

Data Limitations

Coverage across the six countries is uneven—particularly for Bosnia and Herzegovina (BH), North Macedonia (NM), and Montenegro—and this is most evident in the emissions series (Chart 5). Historical, country-level series preceding the breakup of Yugoslavia are also limited; for example, 1990 values could not be included for Chart 2 due to missing data.

For Charts 3 and 6, consistent and fully aligned indicators for all target countries were not available from higher-confidence sources, so Wikipedia tables were used to fill gaps. Because the page contained multiple tables with varying headers and some unnamed sections, initial Python parsing returned mixed ranges. To ensure consistency, all fields were standardized and cleaned in Excel before visualization.

Conclusion

Slovenia and Croatia display stronger development outcomes relative to peers. Despite having the second-highest GDP, Serbia faces persistently higher unemployment than Croatia and Slovenia. Bosnia and Herzegovina’s weaker performance is consistent with the depth of wartime destruction and subsequent political fragmentation. Outcomes in North Macedonia (a landlocked economy) and Montenegro (with high reliance on tourism) align with structural characteristics observed prior to analysis.

A strict causal attribution to conflict legacy alone would be too strong. However, among the three most affected states, the imprint appears most persistent in BH, while Serbia (with historically greater military capacity and eastern alignments) and Croatia (closer western integration and significant tourism assets) seem to have mitigated a larger share of legacy constraints.
